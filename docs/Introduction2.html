0.1 Introduction (W.I.P)

Main things that need to change

  • Emphasise the SN-perspective of the topic

  • Just writing basically…

Inequalities are omnipresent in society. Not all people have the same opportunities and thus social differences exist in who has and who has not. One of the most prominent social divisions in society is gender, with terms such as the “glass-ceiling” and the “gender wage-gap” that are familiar to many (ref). Yet, even after many efforts to make policy changes, women are still not on equal footing to men. Unfortunately, the academic world is not an outlier in this case. Within the science of science, much has been written about academic disparities between men and women (ref.). Even within the social sciences, full professors are more often men than women and wage gaps still exist (vox??). At the same time, gender norms are becoming more egalitarian and within the university context, many policies have been installed towards more equality among male and female scholars. If the structures in which the academic world operates is becoming more egalitarian, one could wonder how these inequalities are kept in place. In this paper, I argue that inequalities in academic prestige are kept in place due to the social relations between scholars.

. In a study that looked at the impact that networks have on gender differences in coauthorship, it was found that representation of women matters in relation to their network access (McDowell, Singell, and Stater 2006). The study looked at how male and female economist PhD’s have access in their networks for publications and co-authorship. They find that over time, women are less likely to coauthor and publish articles compared to men. However, they also find that this probability does dwindle by 1993. When other forms of gender differences are included, women are less likely to be “successful” in academics. Even within the social sciences, men and women do not receive the same treatment and grants, publications and upper academic positions are unequally distributed (Bellotti et al. 2022). This concept of academic inequality has been called the Matilda effect, named after the Matthew effect, named by Robert Merton. Bellotti et al. (2022) studied the composition of male and female collaboration networks in Italy. They find persisting inequalities between men and women in terms of research funding and collaboration across all academic disciplines. They argue that their findings are generalisable to fields where collaboration is key to success.
  From a scientific standpoint, very little studies within the collaboration literature have focused on clustering in terms of social inequalities. (Long, Cunningham, and Braithwaite 2013) wrote a systematic review on brokers in collaborative networks and how this influence the spread of information. Their conclusion is that while brokers, bridges and boundary spanners do play a critical role in spreading information throughout networks, information will spread more efficiently when networks are more close knit. However, this paper and thus the articles it has reviewed, pay little attention to who these brokers are. In organisational science, organisations are assumed to be egalitarian and thus social inequalities are deemed non existent. This study will drop this assumption in order to see whether gender inequalities play a role in the collaborative networks of academics. Specifically, this article will study how coauthorship networks of Dutch Sociologists are clustered in terms of gender.
  Secondly, this article will look at how this gender clustering in coauthorship networks influences the prestige or success of female scholars. Previous studies pointed out that not all research is valued equally and that gender is still an indicator of this inequality (Bellotti et al. 2022; Bozeman and Gaughan 2011). By studying this inequality through a social network perspective, this study will help us to see how male and female scholars relate to each other and how there preferences influence the likelihood of publishing together (or not), and will give insights into the patterns of gaining academic prestige (or not). This leads to the following research question:

  • RQ 1: To what extent does genderhomophily influence clustering of collaboration networks in terms of Sociologists at Radboud University and Utrecht University?

  • RQ 2: To what extent does gender clustering of publication networks influence the prestige/success of female scholars at Radboud University and Utrecht University?

    Alternative

  • RQ 1: To what extent has intergender-collaboration in collaboration networks of sociologists at Radboud university and Utrecht University changed over time?

  • RQ 2: To what extent does genderhomophily in collaboration networks influence the prestige/success of female sociologists?
